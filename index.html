How reliable are air quality measurements?

The WHYs and WHATs of air quality and its quantification

Air is essentially one of the most important resources for our body and rightly so, air quality has been a target for today’s media. The air we breathe can carry some harmful particles called PM or Particulate Matter. They are broadly categorised into PM10 and PM 2.5 corresponding to particulate matter smaller than 10 micrometers and 2.5 micrometers respectively. To put it in numbers, an average human being breaths around 12-16 times per minute during normal activity. This corresponds to approximately 13 kg of air daily. Out of the recommended standard for PM inhalation by WHO, we reach about 75% of that limit daily and this number will continue to grow with more urbanization and industrialization. This also affects indoor air quality.Unfortunately, these particles have major effects on health, causing cancer, inflammation to premature deaths (WHO 2013).

We are notified about the air quality around us through measurement instruments placed all around us. These instruments, however carry some uncertainties. These can be broadly divided into three categories:

  • Measurement uncertainty - The capability and accuracy of the instrument varies with its hardware quality. A cheaper instrument may give flawed measurements.

  • Temporal uncertainty - A calculated average of all values of measurements does not really reflect the true situation. An hourly average can be misleading.

  • Spatial uncertainty - A fixed instrument at a location might not be able to accurately measure air quality at the other end of the city. One value for an entire city can be misleading as well.

Measurement Uncertainty

Measurement instruments like this have been deployed all over Europe to assess and control the quality of air. But the fact that these instruments are neither cheap nor small prohibits easy and widespread deployment. As is with all machines, the accuracy of these measurements varies.



For the state of North Rhine-Westphalia, you can see an overview of the hourly updated measured values of various components on the LANUV website and a calculated air quality index on aqicn.org.



But that’s not all.

The Institute of Geoinformatics at University of Muenster has their citizen science project called Sensebox, where they provide Do-It-Yourself air quality measurement kits called SenseBox. It’s a self assembly kit capable of measuring PM10 and PM2.5 with a wide range of sensors for ambient lighting, temperature, barometric pressure, etc.



This instrument can be purchased and deployed outside individual homes and the data can be shared using their openSenseMap, an open source platform for sharing sensebox data. It is a publicly affordable version of the LANUV stations. But the hardware introduces some uncertainty and can be less accurate than a full-powered LANUV station.



Along with such initiatives, there are researchers working on measuring air quality.

An example would be the Landscape ecology group at the University of Muenster has come up with a mobile solution for measuring air quality. They retrofitted a bicycle with an air quality measurement unit measuring different kinds of particles.



The bike can be ridden around the city and can measure particles as well as temperature and pressure. As the bike is capable of carrying a larger load, the sensors fitted are of a better quality than the sensebox but still not as powerful as the LANUV.

All in all, three levels of measurement instruments can be identified. Sensebox as the most affordable and therefore easily deployable providing a large network of sensors, The bike as more of a research oriented but a mobile sensor and LANUV as the industrial sensor used for official purposes.



Together they can make up a reliable network of air quality measurement instruments but other uncertainties still prevail.
