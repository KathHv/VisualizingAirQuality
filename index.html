Air Quality: How much can we trust official data?

Investigating PM10 data for Münster

Air quality and its effects has been a target for today’s media. The air we breathe does carry some harmful particles called PM or Particulate Matter. They are broadly categorised into PM10 and PM 2.5 corresponding to particulate matter smaller than 10 micrometers and 2.5 micrometers respectively. To put it in numbers, an average human inhales around 12-16 times per minute. This corresponds to approximately 13 kg of air daily. Out of the recommended standard for PM inhalation by World Health Organization (WHO), we reach about 75% of that limit daily and this number will continue to grow with more urbanization and industrialization. This also affects indoor air quality. Unfortunately, these particles have major effects on our health. A 2013 report by WHO states, a poor ambient air quality can cause cancer, inflammation to premature deaths.


We are notified about the air quality around us through measurement instruments deployed in various parts of our cities. But the measurements reported have some uncertainties or errors associated with them. These can be broadly divided into three categories:


Measurement (hardware-based) uncertainty
The capability and accuracy of the instrument varies with its hardware quality. A cheaper instrument may give flawed measurements. Temporal (time-based) uncertainty
A calculated average of all values of measurements over one hour or a day does not really reflect the true measurement at a point in time. An hourly average can be misleading. Spatial (distance-based) uncertainty
A fixed instrument at a location might not be able to accurately measure air quality at the other end of the city. One value for an entire city can be misleading as well.

Measurement Uncertainty

Measurement instruments like this have been deployed all over Europe to assess and control the quality of air. But the fact that these instruments are neither cheap nor small prohibits easy and widespread deployment. As is with all machines, the accuracy of these measurements varies.


For the state of North Rhine-Westphalia, you can see an overview of the hourly updated measured values of various components on the LANUV website and a calculated air quality index on aqicn.org.


But that’s not all.

The Institute of Geoinformatics at University of Muenster has their citizen science project called SenseBox, where they provide Do-It-Yourself air quality measurement kits.It is capable of measuring PM10 and PM2.5 with a wide range of sensors configurations.

The data can be shared using their openSenseMap. It is a publicly affordable version of the LANUV stations. But the qualty of hardware introduces some uncertainty and can be less accurate than a full-powered LANUV station.


Along with such initiatives, there are researchers working on measuring air quality.

An example would be the Landscape ecology group at the University of Muenster and their mobile air quality measurement instrument. They have retrofitted a bicycle with an air quality measurement unit measuring different kinds of particles.

This bike can be ridden around the city and can measure air quality. As the bike is capable of carrying a larger load, the sensors quality is between Sensebox and LANUV. But the bike has a power limitation since it relies on Lithium batteries for sensors.

All in all, three levels of measurement instruments can be identified. Sensebox as the most affordable, The bike as a mobile sensor and LANUV as the industrial sensor used for official purposes. Together they can make up a reliable network of air quality measurement instruments.

But other uncertainties still prevail.

Temporal (time-based) Uncertainty

Time:

Official LANUV stations only publish data averaged for one hour. But it just so happens that the air quality around you can be disrupted by ,for example, the vehicles driving around you. This is an instantaneous change not reflected in measurements taken by a station at the other end of the city.

The map shows two stations in Muenster with the values of PM10 through out the day as you scroll the timer above changes and so do the measurements. Different colors show different values. The scale on the bottom right shows the range of measurements.

We know PM 2.5 are considered more dangerous than PM 10 but the stations in Muenster do not measure PM2.5 at all.

Therefore, We used the bike to cover the area around one of the stations (Muenster-Geist) to understand if the air quality differs as we move away from the station. We rode around smaller and larger streets, parks and some traffic-dense areas.

We found a considerable rise and fall in PM10 values for streets around the station. Some anomalies were introduced by cars passing by corresponding to the high values on map. Officially, we have just a single value of PM10 for the entire bike ride to compare to. You can see how much the values vary.

Time:

For a more consistant comparision, we also used a sensebox at a fixed location next to the LANUV station. The sensebox measures PM10 every second but is also stationary. This made it a perfect crossover between LANUV and the bike. As seen here, the PM10 values measured by sensebox also vary a lot in one hour.

Time:

Through out study, We found that LANUV stations measure accurately but can't measure the air quality at the other end of the city. The bike can measure at various locations but it only measures once at a location and moves on. It's also not possible for the bike to be driven continuously. A sensebox can be a preliminary source if enough of them are deployed but sensor quality matters. But if all are represented together, they can be a reliable source of information.

Spatial (distance-based) Uncertainty

Imagine you are standing at the red spot and you want to know the air quality. A simplistic approach would be to check the measurement at the nearest station, say the one at Weseler Str. Now this is a pretty busy street. But you have no cars going around you. The measurement you get would be way higher than it is at your location.
